## Milwaukee Public Radio Executive Builds Stock Market Simulator Where Black Culture IS the Economy

**BLK Exchange uses 36 fictional Black-economy companies and AI coaching to teach investing through gameplay, not lectures**

**Milwaukee, WI, March 30, 2026** -- Tarik Moody has launched BLK Exchange, a stock market simulator built for Black Americans who want to learn investing but have never found a tool that speaks their language. Players trade 36 fictional companies across 12 sectors of the Black economy, from a natural hair care brand (CROWN) to a Black-owned streaming platform (VIZN) to a community bank (VAULT), while AI coaches them through 23 real investing concepts.

Black culture is an economy. BLK Exchange treats it like one.

### The gap it fills

Black Americans answer 37% of financial literacy questions correctly, compared to 55% for White Americans, according to the TIAA Institute-GFLEC Personal Finance Index. Only 18% of Black households own stocks, bonds, or mutual funds. For every $100 in wealth held by White families, Black families hold $15.

The tools that exist haven't moved those numbers. Generic stock simulators trade Apple and Tesla with Bloomberg news. They assume a familiarity with the stock market that millions of Black Americans don't have, not because of any lack of intelligence, but because the tools were never designed for them.

"People watch Earn Your Leisure, follow finance accounts on social media, absorb the information," Moody said. "But consumption doesn't translate to practice. There's nowhere to actually try investing in a context that feels familiar. That's what BLK Exchange is."

### Why cultural relevance matters

The approach behind BLK Exchange is called Culturally Responsive Education. The research on it is clear. NYU's Metropolitan Center for Research on Equity found that Black students who received culturally relevant teaching had better academic outcomes. A study published in School Science and Mathematics found that Black students engaged more deeply with mathematical concepts when they were embedded in African American cultural stories.

The same logic applies to investing. When a player reads that CROWN stock is surging after a natural hair care partnership, they're processing a financial event through a lens they already understand. The company feels real because the world it represents is real. That's something a simulator built around Apple and Tesla will never do.

A 2024 randomized controlled trial across four countries found that game-based financial education measurably improved financial literacy, and that adapting the content to reflect cultural differences made it work better. BLK Exchange was designed around that finding.

### How it works

Players start with $10,000 in virtual cash and trade companies like KICKS (athletic footwear), DRIP (streetwear), PIXL (a Black-owned game studio), and BLOK (a real estate investment trust). Real cultural news from Black media publications drives the market. When a headline about a streaming deal or a sneaker collaboration drops, stock prices move.

Two AI systems run during gameplay. Groq, a fast inference engine, generates market events and classifies incoming news to specific companies and investing concepts. Claude, built by Anthropic, coaches players through portfolio decisions, answers investing questions using their actual holdings as examples, and writes personalized session debriefs.

The system tracks which of the 23 concepts each player hasn't encountered yet and generates events targeting those gaps. Moody calls it the "curriculum debt queue." The result: players don't miss concepts because the game won't let them.

### Built by a non-developer in a week

Moody is not a software developer. He built the entire platform in roughly a week using Claude Code, an AI coding tool from Anthropic. No traditional programming background. He directed the AI to architect a real-time backend with a 17-table database, a dual AI coaching system, a news pipeline that scrapes Black media publications, and a mobile-installable web app. It runs on Convex, Clerk, and Vercel.

"A year ago this would have taken a team of engineers months to build," Moody said. "I built it in a week by describing what I wanted and letting the AI write the code. That's the world we're in now."

### Built for Hackonomics 2026

BLK Exchange was built for [Hackonomics 2026](https://hackonomics26.devpost.com/), a national hackathon that challenges participants to fuse computer science and economics into projects that spread financial literacy awareness in their communities. The hackathon runs from September 2025 through March 30, 2026, and judges submissions on relevancy to financial literacy, technical execution, presentation, real-world impact, and innovation.

The project is accompanied by a research paper citing 28 peer-reviewed studies.

### From sim to real: Black-owned companies are on the stock market

Most people don't know this: only 8 Black-owned companies are publicly traded on major U.S. exchanges. Eight. Out of thousands. Companies like Urban One (UONE), Carver Bancorp (CARV), Broadway Financial (BYFC), and Direct Digital Holdings (DRCT). There is one ETF designed in partnership with a civil rights organization, the NAACP Minority Empowerment ETF (NACP).

BLK Exchange plans to bridge the gap between simulation and reality. A feature in development called The Real Exchange will introduce those 8 real companies alongside the 36 fictional tickers once a player proves they understand the fundamentals: 15 concepts unlocked, a strong diversification score, trades across multiple sectors. The real tickers show up right next to the sim tickers. LOUD sits next to Urban One. VAULT sits next to Carver Bancorp. The connection is immediate.

The capstone is NACP. When a player buys it, they go from picking individual stocks to building a diversified portfolio that reflects their values. The AI coach says: "You've been trading the sim. Now meet the companies that inspired it."

The education pipeline doesn't end at "you learned something." It ends at "you're actually investing."

### Who it's for

College students at HBCUs. High school students learning about money for the first time. Working adults in their 20s and 30s who know they should be investing but feel like the existing apps weren't made for them. The app is in public beta and under active development, with social sharing, investment clubs, and expanded AI coaching planned for the coming months. It is free and available now at blkexchange.com.

### Supporting the project

BLK Exchange is free and will stay free. Moody is self-funding the project, which runs on AI services (Claude, Groq, ElevenLabs), real-time infrastructure (Convex), and cloud hosting (Vercel), all of which cost money. He is accepting donations to cover those costs and continued development through Buy Me a Coffee at [buymeacoffee.com/tarikmoody](https://buymeacoffee.com/tarikmoody).

"I'm not charging users. The whole point is to remove barriers to financial literacy, not add new ones," Moody said. "But the AI that powers the coaching, the news pipeline, the debriefs, that costs real money to run. If people believe in the mission, they can help keep it alive."

### About Tarik Moody

Tarik Moody is the Director of Strategy and Innovation at Radio Milwaukee, an NPR member station, and a Howard University graduate (School of Architecture, Class of 1996). He has spent his career in media strategy and design, not software engineering. He built BLK Exchange using AI coding tools because he couldn't find the financial literacy tool he wanted for his own community. BLK Exchange is his entry in the Hackonomics 2026 hackathon.
