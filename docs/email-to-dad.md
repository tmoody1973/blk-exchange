Hey Dad,

I wanted to tell you about something I've been building. I entered a hackathon called Hackenomics and built an app called BLK Exchange. The deadline is tomorrow, but I think you'll appreciate what it's about more than most people.

You know how you've always talked about Black folks needing to get into investing? The numbers back you up. Black families hold $15 for every $100 White families hold. Only 18% of Black households own any stocks at all. And here's the part that really got to me when I was researching this: even Black people with college degrees score lower on financial literacy than White people with the same education. It's not about intelligence or effort. The system just wasn't built to teach us.

That's the problem I'm trying to solve.

BLK Exchange is a stock market game. You start with $10,000 in virtual cash and trade 36 fictional companies that represent the Black economy. Not Apple and Tesla. Companies like CROWN (a natural hair care brand), KICKS (an athletic footwear company), DRIP (a streetwear label), VIZN (a Black-owned streaming platform), VAULT (a community bank). Twelve sectors total, three companies each.

Here's what makes it different from anything else out there. Real cultural news moves the market. When a headline drops about a streaming deal or a sneaker collaboration, the stock prices change. And when they change, an AI coach explains what just happened and what investing concept you just experienced. Not a lecture. Not a textbook definition. An explanation tied to the trade you just made, using the companies you actually care about.

There are 23 investing concepts built into the game. Diversification, P/E ratios, sector correlation, risk tolerance, earnings reports, all the way up to generational wealth. You don't unlock them by reading. You unlock them by doing. If you hold companies across 4 different sectors, the game recognizes you just practiced diversification and teaches you the concept. If a market event wipes out one sector and your portfolio survives because you were spread out, the game shows you why that matters.

By the time someone plays through a few sessions, they've learned real investing fundamentals without ever feeling like they were being taught.

I wrote a research paper to go with it. 28 peer-reviewed citations on why this approach works. Three things the research says: game-based learning improves financial literacy by a measurable amount. Culturally relevant teaching produces better outcomes for Black students. And structured curriculum through play beats lectures and worksheets every time. BLK Exchange is all three at once.

The people I see using this first are college students at HBCUs, high school kids who watch Earn Your Leisure but have never opened a brokerage account, and working adults in their 20s and 30s who know they should be investing but feel like the existing apps weren't made for them. Because they weren't.

I built the whole thing in about a week with AI coding tools. It's live right now. Real-time market, AI coaching, the full 36-company universe, leaderboards, a Knowledge Vault that tracks what you've learned. It runs on your phone like an app.

I'd love for you to try it and tell me what you think. And if you know anyone in the financial literacy space, the education world, or community organizations who might want to see this, I'd appreciate the introduction.
